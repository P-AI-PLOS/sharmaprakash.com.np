In 2012, CD Projekt Red announced Cyberpunk 2077 with the most agile sentence a studio can say in public: it'll be ready when it's ready. No date, no promise, just an intention held open until reality filled it in. Eight years later, that same project announced a release date of April 16, 2020 — and then moved it to September 17, then to November 19, then to December 10. Three delays, publicly, inside a single year, each one a press release admitting the plan was wrong. And here's the thing that makes it worth closing this series on: **every delay was the organization receiving information, and not one of them was the organization responding to it.** The date moved. Nothing attached to the date — scope, platforms, marketing commitments, the promise of a humane schedule — moved with it. The gap between the plan and reality had to be paid somehow, and it was paid twice: first by the people building the game, then by the people who bought it.

I want to be clear about the spirit of this post before going further. CD Projekt Red made one of my favorite games of the previous decade, the failure I'm describing is a system failure that plenty of organizations replicate at smaller scale with less press coverage, and the individuals involved were, by every account, talented people working very hard. That's precisely what makes it useful. Nobody here was lazy or stupid. They followed the plan.

## What does a delay actually change?

Less than it appears to. A delay announcement reads like a response to change — we've learned the date was wrong, so we've moved it. But look at what stayed fixed around Cyberpunk's three moves. The scope stayed fixed: the same enormous open world, on the full platform list, including the aging PS4 and Xbox One hardware that would eventually sink the launch. The marketing stayed fixed, and kept compounding — partnership deals, merchandising, and the Keanu Reeves reveal at E3 2019, a moment so successful it converted the release date from a project estimate into a public event with commercial machinery bolted to it. Each delay reset the clock without resetting anything the clock was measuring.

That's the pattern worth naming, because it's not unique to games. [The manifesto's fourth value](/product-management/the-agile-manifesto-four-values-twelve-principles/) — responding to change over following a plan — is routinely misread as *don't plan*, but its actual demand is harder: when evidence invalidates the plan, change what you're doing, not just what you're saying. A moved date with unmoved scope isn't a response; it's a deferral. The team is still being asked to do the same amount of work in not-enough time — the not-enough has just been rescheduled. Three delays should have been three opportunities to renegotiate the triangle of scope, quality, and date. Instead, all three were resolved the same way, and it's the way the triangle always resolves when nobody will touch scope on purpose: it came out of the people. Sonos ran the mirror image of this failure with its 2024 app rewrite — the date held and the product gave — and [shipped the date instead of the app](/product-management/sonos-shipped-the-date-not-the-app/).

## Where did the schedule gap actually go?

Into evenings and weekends, mostly. In 2019, co-founder Marcin Iwiński publicly committed the studio to a "non-obligatory crunch" policy — a notable promise in an industry where crunch is the unspoken default, and by the accounts of people who worked there, sincerely meant. In 2020, Jason Schreier's reporting at Bloomberg revealed that the studio had made six-day weeks mandatory in the final stretch, and developers described crunch that had, for some, stretched over a year. The promise didn't break because leadership was cynical. It broke because it was structurally impossible: a fixed date, fixed scope, and a humane schedule are three commitments, and the organization had only ever been willing to renegotiate zero of them in public. Something had to give, and the schedule promise was the only one without a marketing campaign attached.

The opening post of this series argued that [sustainable pace is an engineering claim, not a kindness](/product-management/the-agile-manifesto-four-values-twelve-principles/), and Cyberpunk is the argument at industrial scale. The manifesto's eighth principle uses the word *indefinitely* — a constant pace the team can maintain indefinitely — and the reason that's engineering rather than welfare is visible in what a year of six-day weeks produces. Exhausted people don't produce less software at the same quality; they produce software at a normal-looking rate with a defect density that arrives as a delayed invoice. The bugs that crunch produces eat the schedule that crunch was supposed to save — and on this project, the invoice came due on the largest possible stage. A year of overtime bought three moved dates and still shipped a game that didn't work. If crunch were the productivity tool organizations treat it as, this is the project where it would have worked. It's the project that proves it doesn't.

## The launch: what happens when trailer footage is the measure of progress

On December 10, 2020, the game shipped — and on the last-generation consoles that represented a huge share of preorders, it was fundamentally broken. Not rough-edged; broken, in the crashes-and-missing-world sense, to a degree that the years of gorgeous trailer footage had given no hint of. Reporting afterward indicated the console performance problems were known internally before launch — the warnings existed; the plan outranked them. A week later came a sentence I never expected to read about a AAA release: on December 17, Sony removed Cyberpunk 2077 from the PlayStation Store entirely and offered blanket refunds. Investor lawsuits followed. Leadership publicly conceded they had underestimated the risk and ignored the warning signs.

The manifesto's sharpest sentence — *working software is the primary measure of progress* — sounds banal until you watch an organization run on a different measure. For eight years, Cyberpunk's public measure of progress was rendered footage: trailers, demos, the E3 moment. Footage is a *document* — a claim about what the software will do, produced separately from whether it does it — and this is [the FBI Sentinel failure](/product-management/fbi-sentinel-working-software/) wearing a different costume: a decade of impressive artifacts standing in for the one artifact that can't be faked, running software on the actual target hardware in front of someone allowed to say it doesn't work. Sony's delisting was, in a grim way, the first honest demo the last-gen version ever got. The customer collaboration the manifesto points at — an ongoing, truthful conversation with players and platform holders about what would actually ship, on which hardware, in what state — was available at every one of the three delays. Each time, the organization chose the announcement over the conversation, and in the end the platform holder held the retro in public.

## The epilogue is the sharpest part

Because here's what happened next. CD Projekt Red spent years patching the game. The Edgerunners anime in 2022 sent players back to it and they found something worth returning to. The Phantom Liberty expansion in 2023 landed to genuine acclaim, and the game is now, by broad consensus, excellent — commercially and critically redeemed. Which reveals the uncomfortable truth underneath the whole story: **the product was always going to be good eventually. The fiction was never the game; it was the date.** Given time, the studio's ambition and talent produced exactly what the trailers promised. What the organization could not do — what it structurally refused to do, three separate times — was tell the truth about *when*, or cut scope to make a nearer truth possible.

Run the tape back and count how the scope-quality-date triangle got resolved. Three times by crunch. Once by shipping broken. And never — not until after launch, when the last-gen versions were eventually dropped from the expansion entirely — by the move the manifesto's tenth principle names as essential: simplicity, the art of maximizing the amount of work not done. A smaller launch scope, an honest platform cut, a renegotiated commitment with the customer while it was still cheap — every one of those options was cheaper than what actually happened, and every one of them was unspeakable, because the plan and its marketing machinery had made cutting feel like breach rather than steering. The delays prove the organization *could* absorb bad news. It just kept absorbing it into the wrong variable.

## Three stories, four values, zero frameworks

This is where the series lands, so let me put the three stories side by side. [FBI Sentinel](/product-management/fbi-sentinel-working-software/) is *working software over comprehensive documentation*: half a billion dollars of documents and status reports, rescued by a team that made running software the only currency that counted. [The Spotify model](/product-management/the-spotify-model-spotify-doesnt-use/) is *individuals and interactions over processes and tools*: a snapshot of one company's people-first thinking, calcified by everyone else into the exact process-worship it was describing an escape from — the practices copied, the principle left behind. And Cyberpunk 2077 is the other two values: *responding to change over following a plan*, declined three times at compounding cost, and *customer collaboration over contract negotiation*, where the contract was an announced date with a marketing campaign for a signature, and the collaboration finally happened involuntarily, through a storefront delisting.

Notice what none of these stories contains: a framework. Sentinel wasn't saved by Scrum certification, Spotify's insight wasn't the squad diagram, and no ceremony on earth would have fixed Cyberpunk's launch — the studio didn't lack process, it lacked the willingness to let reality outrank the plan. Which has been the argument of this whole series, from [the reread of the manifesto](/product-management/the-agile-manifesto-four-values-twelve-principles/) through [what a team looks like running on principles alone](/product-management/agile-without-scrum-or-kanban/): the entire operating core is four priority calls and twelve principles, written down in 2001, readable in four minutes. Everything else — the boards, the sprints, the scaled frameworks, the squads and tribes — is packaging, sometimes helpful and always optional. The priorities are not optional, and the evidence isn't ideological, it's forensic: organizations that invert them don't get caught by auditors. They get caught by reality, which holds the retro whether you schedule it or not.

## Put it to work

1. **Find your organization's unmovable variable.** Next time a date slips, watch what moves with it. If the date changes but scope, staffing, and external promises all stay fixed, the gap hasn't been closed — it's been assigned, silently, to the team's evenings or the product's quality. Ask, in the replanning meeting, the question nobody asked in 2020: *which commitment are we renegotiating to make the new date true?* "None" is an answer; it's just an answer that names who's paying.
2. **Audit your measure of progress for trailer footage.** List what your stakeholders actually see between commitments — decks, demos on the fast hardware, staged walkthroughs, burndown charts. Anything that isn't the real software in its worst supported environment is footage, and the gap between footage and reality compounds quietly until launch day prices it. Demo on the oldest hardware, the slowest connection, the ugliest data you support.
3. **Price the cut you're not allowed to discuss.** Every plan under pressure has a scope cut that would relieve it — a platform, a feature tier, a launch market — that's become unspeakable because something public is attached to it. Write down what keeping it is costing per month in overtime and deferred quality, and put the number next to the cost of the awkward announcement. Cyberpunk's math said the announcement was cheaper three separate times. Yours probably does too.
